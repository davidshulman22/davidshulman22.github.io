The documents should serve the plan.

A sound estate plan begins with what you own, who depends on you, how decisions should be made if you cannot make them, and what you want to happen over time. The documents are the legal machinery for those decisions—not the starting point.

For some families, a coordinated will and incapacity plan is enough. Others need revocable or irrevocable trusts, tax planning, asset-protection analysis, business succession work, or carefully structured provisions for children and beneficiaries with special needs.

Common planning components

  • Last will and testament
  • Revocable living trust
  • Durable power of attorney
  • Designation of health care surrogate and living will
  • Beneficiary-designation and asset-titling review
  • Planning for minor children or special-needs beneficiaries
  • Estate, gift, and generation-skipping tax planning
  • Asset protection and business succession coordination

When to revisit the plan

Review is especially important after a marriage or divorce, a birth or death, a significant change in assets, a move to Florida, a business transaction, a health change, or a shift in the people you trust to act for you.

What the first meeting should accomplish

The first meeting should identify the legal and practical issues, expose gaps or conflicts in the current arrangement, and define the work needed to create or update the plan. You should leave understanding the recommended direction and why it fits.
