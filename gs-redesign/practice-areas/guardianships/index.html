Incapacity and Vulnerable Adult Protection

Guardianship

Court-supervised protection when a loved one can no longer manage their own care, finances, or safety and no less restrictive alternative is enough.

Guardianship asks a court to determine that someone lacks legal capacity and to appoint a guardian to make decisions on their behalf.

When Guardianship Is Necessary

The first question is whether guardianship is actually required. A valid power of attorney, trust, or health care surrogate designation can sometimes avoid court involvement. When existing documents are defective, unavailable, or being misused, guardianship may be the only workable protection.

Contested and Uncontested Proceedings

Some cases move forward without meaningful dispute. Others involve conflict over capacity, who should serve as guardian, or whether a less restrictive alternative exists. Contested matters require realistic expectations about timeline, cost, and court involvement.

Guardian Responsibilities

A guardian may be responsible for decisions about residence, medical care, daily welfare, assets, bills, investments, accountings, and annual plans. The role carries real reporting obligations and potential personal liability.

Vulnerable Adult Exploitation

Financial exploitation often shows up before a family knows what legal tool is needed. The work begins with stopping the harm, preserving records, and choosing the right court intervention.
