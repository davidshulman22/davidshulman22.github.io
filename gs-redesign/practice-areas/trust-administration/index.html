Florida Trustees and Beneficiaries

Trust Administration

Guidance for successor trustees and beneficiaries after a trust becomes irrevocable, from required notices and accountings through disputes, distributions, and closing.

Trust administration is private, but it is not automatic. A trustee has fiduciary duties and can be personally liable for getting the process wrong.

After the Settlor Dies

A revocable trust usually becomes irrevocable at death and the successor trustee steps in. The trustee must locate the trust, identify which assets are actually in the trust, give required notices, preserve records, and begin administration under the Florida Trust Code.

There is no court automatically supervising the process. That can make trust administration faster and more private than probate, but it also means the trustee has to know what the law and the document require.

Trustee Duties

Trustees must act loyally, invest prudently, keep beneficiaries reasonably informed, account when required, pay proper expenses, and distribute according to the trust document. A family trustee is still a fiduciary, even if they are also a beneficiary.

Beneficiary Rights

Qualified beneficiaries may be entitled to notice, a copy of the trust on request, accountings, and relevant information about trust assets and liabilities. If a trustee fails to account, commingles assets, makes improper distributions, or refuses to provide information, beneficiaries may need court relief.

When Probate Is Still Needed

If assets were left outside the trust, probate may run alongside trust administration. The personal representative and trustee may be the same person, or they may be different people with different duties. Coordinating both processes avoids delays, creditor problems, and inconsistent decisions.
