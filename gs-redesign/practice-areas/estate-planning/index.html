Estate Planning

Florida estate planning that accounts for homestead, incapacity, asset titling, beneficiary designations, tax concerns, and the people who will have to use the documents later.

Estate planning decides who controls your finances if you cannot, who makes medical decisions for you, and how your assets transfer at death without leaving those questions to a court.

What a Florida Plan Should Cover

A complete Florida plan usually includes a last will and testament or revocable trust, a durable power of attorney, a designation of health care surrogate, and a living will. More complex plans may include irrevocable trusts, special needs provisions, planning for minor children, business succession structures, or tax planning.

The documents are only part of the work. Beneficiary designations, account ownership, deeds, and trust funding often decide whether the plan actually works.

Florida Homestead

Homestead is one of the most common planning traps in Florida. The constitution can restrict how a primary residence passes when there is a surviving spouse or minor child, and those rules can override what a will says. In second marriages, the result can be very different from what either spouse expected.

Homestead also connects estate planning to guardianship. If a home passes to a minor child outright, a court-supervised guardianship of the property may be required until the child turns eighteen.

When a Trust Makes Sense

A revocable trust can avoid probate when it is properly funded. If the trust is signed but assets are never moved into it, the family may still need a probate administration. For Florida residents with property in more than one state, a funded trust can also avoid multiple probate proceedings.
